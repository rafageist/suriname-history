# Topic 4: Our Country During World War II

## Lesson 1: War with Germany

When groups within a country or two or more countries fight against each other, we speak of a war. When in a fight many countries (in different continents) are involved, we speak of a world war. Such large wars fortunately do not occur often. Yet there have been two world wars in the 20th century. We call them World War I and World War II.

World War II began in Europe in 1939. The war ended in 1945. During this war, different European countries fought against each other. Other countries also became involved in this war. The countries can be divided into two groups. On the one side were Germany, Italy, and Japan. The other group consisted of England, France, North America, Russia, and China. Both groups received support from other countries.

The Netherlands was occupied by Germany during World War II. And because our country at that time was still a colony of the Netherlands, Suriname was then also at war with Germany. During the war years, the government of our country was in the hands of Governor Kielstra. When the Netherlands was occupied by the Germans in May 1940, the state of siege was proclaimed in our country. With this, the governor received all power. He could make decisions alone, without needing permission from others.

One of the decisions Governor Kielstra made at that time was that Germans who lived in our country were arrested and interned in a prison camp on Copieweg. Most Germans had already lived in our country for a long time and had ordinary professions such as teacher or merchant. Suddenly they were enemies.

#### REMEMBER

- In a world war, many countries in different continents are involved.
- During World War II, the Netherlands was occupied by Germany. As a Dutch colony, our country was then also at war with Germany.
- Governor Kielstra proclaimed the state of siege in Suriname in 1940.
- The Germans in our country were locked up in a prison camp.
- The German ship, the Goslar, was scuttled by the helmsman in the Suriname River. The wreck lies there to this day.
- In Jodensavanne, 146 men were imprisoned. They were prisoners from the Dutch colony of the East Indies. They were suspected of treason and were very badly treated.

In the harbor of Paramaribo, there was also a German ship, the Goslar. In September 1939, this cargo ship had sailed up the Suriname River, for fear of an attack at sea. The German crew stayed in Suriname. They too were arrested in May 1940 and locked up. The governor gave the order to seize the ship, but the helmsman of the ship had opened a hatch the evening before. Water flowed into the ship, and it listed; it capsized. To this day, the wreck still lies in the Suriname River. Part of it still sticks out above the water.

In 1942, 146 men arrived in our country from the Dutch colony of the East Indies. They had been imprisoned there as traitors. The East Indies was occupied by Japan in 1942, and the Netherlands decided to transfer these prisoners to Suriname. The governor did not receive papers that could prove that these men were really traitors. But he also could not just let them go free. Therefore, a camp was set up at Jodensavanne where these men were imprisoned and obligated to work. Around the camp were three rows of barbed wire, and at every corner was a watchtower. Only in July 1946 were the remaining men freed, and they left by ship to the Netherlands.
