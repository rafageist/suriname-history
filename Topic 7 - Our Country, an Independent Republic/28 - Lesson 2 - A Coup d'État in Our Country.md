### Student Textbook Content

A Coup d'État in Our Country

The Surinamese Armed Forces in 1975

At independence in 1975, our country also received its own army. The Dutch troops, the TRIS, were abolished, and the Dutch military personnel returned to the Netherlands. Instead, the Surinamese Armed Forces were established with Surinamese military personnel. Also, Surinamese military personnel who had returned from the Netherlands could enlist. The buildings, weapons, and vehicles of the TRIS were transferred to the Surinamese Armed Forces. Later, the name of the Surinamese Armed Forces was changed to National Army.

In the first years after independence, problems in our country became clearer. For example, there was still much unemployment and poverty. The housing shortage was also still very high. In many households, water hardly came from the tap, electricity often went out, and the telephone often did not work. Among the population, there was much unrest and dissatisfaction. For the government, it was difficult to govern the country well because the different political parties and parliament members often did not agree with each other.

In 1979, the dissatisfaction in our country was great. There were protests against the government from political parties, the population, and the unions. There was also protest from the new armed forces.

The military personnel believed that they, like other employees, were allowed to unite in a union. This union could then advocate for the interests of the military personnel. Therefore, they established the Military Officers' Union (BOMIKA). The government did not want to recognize the union and gave the order in January 1980 to arrest three board members. The three arrested board members would have to appear before the judge on February 26. But that did not happen because on February 25, 1980, the military personnel took over power in our country by means of a coup d'état.

The police building burned down completely

At various buildings, such as the ammunition depot and the Membre Boekoe barracks, there were armed raids. The main police station was shot at and burned to the ground. Only the pillars remained standing. At the place where this building stood, the Revolution Monument was later erected, to commemorate the coup d'État.

Revolution Monument at the Waterkant

The new government in which the military had the leadership said they wanted to bring about a revolution in our country. That means that major changes would come in our society and in the government of our country. During the first years, matters in our country were dealt with firmly. Such as improvement of roads, drainage, and electricity. Also, the establishment of the State Health Insurance and the company Staatsolie took place during this period.

The people and the military would bring progress for our country together

The National Military Council had promised in 1980 that elections would be called within two years. When this had not happened by 1982, the dissatisfaction among the population increased. Conflicts also arose within the army. Actions were taken and there was protest. The military sometimes acted harshly against persons who had a different opinion. The low point of the harsh actions was on December 8, 1982, when fifteen persons who disagreed with the policy met their death.

In the country and abroad, disapproving reactions were given to those events. Development aid from the Netherlands was stopped, and our country faced financial problems. Scarcity of many products arose, and large parts of the population lived in poverty. The popularity of the military among the population decreased.

REMEMBER

- At independence, our country also received its own army, the Surinamese Armed Forces, which later was renamed National Army.
- On February 25, 1980, there was a military coup d'état in our country.
- During the military period, power in our country lay with the Military Authority and the National Military Council.
- At the Waterkant in Paramaribo stands the Revolution Monument. This monument commemorates the military coup d'état of February 25, 1980.
- Against persons who had a different opinion, the military sometimes acted harshly.

### Teacher's Guide - Answers and Explanations

Topic 7 – Our Country, an Independent Republic
A Coup d'État in Our Country

QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS

1. a. In which year was the Surinamese Armed Forces established?
   The Surinamese Armed Forces was established in 1975.
   b. Who could enlist in the Surinamese Armed Forces?
   Surinamese military personnel in our country and Surinamese military personnel who had returned from the Netherlands could enlist.
   c. The name of the Surinamese Armed Forces was later changed to the National Army.

2. Name three problems that the population of our country faced in the first years after independence.
   The problems that the population of our country faced were: unemployment and poverty, high housing shortage, lack of clean drinking water, good electricity, and telephone connections.
   Students only need to write three.

3. Why was there much protest against the government in 1979?
   There was protest against the government because there was much dissatisfaction in our country. The government could not govern the country well because the different political parties and parliament members often did not agree with each other.

4. a. When did the coup d'État take place in our country?
   The coup d'État took place on February 25, 1980.
   b. Explain why the military carried out a coup d'État.
   The military carried out a coup d'État because 3 of their board members were arrested by the government (because the government refused to recognize the union established by the military).

5. Look at the photo. What event does this monument remind us of?
   a. The burning of the police station.
   b. The independence of Suriname.
   c. The establishment of the Surinamese Armed Forces.
   d. The coup d'État of February 25, 1980.

6. Copy the following lines into your notebook. What was the name of the representation of the people in those years?
   1948 – 1975 • •Colonial States
   1975 – 1980 • •National Military Council
   1980 – 1987 • •States of Suriname
   •Parliament of the Republic of Suriname

7. Which answer is correct?
   The highest power in our country lay in the period 1980 – 1987 with ...
   a. the Military Authority.
   b. the Parliament of the Republic of Suriname.
   c. the President.
   d. the people.

8. What does it mean to bring about a revolution?
   That means that major changes would come in our society and in the government of our country.

9. Why did the dissatisfaction of the population increase after the first years of the coup d'État?
   The dissatisfaction of the population increased after the first years because the promised elections did not come.

10. How was reacted in the country and abroad to the harsh actions of the military?
    In the country and abroad, disapproving reactions were given to those events. From the Netherlands, development aid was stopped, and our country faced financial problems. Scarcity of many products arose, and greater poverty prevailed among large parts of the population. The popularity of the military among the population decreased.
