## Lesson 2: The Brokopondo Dam in the Suriname River

### Student Textbook Content

The Brokopondo Dam in the Suriname River

After the Prosperity Fund, the Ten-Year Plan came in 1955. This plan was executed by the Planning Bureau. For the execution of projects included in the Ten-Year Plan, Suriname paid part itself. The other part was paid by the Netherlands.

An important project during the Ten-Year Plan was the Brokopondo Plan. The Surinamese government wanted to build a large hydroelectric power plant in the Suriname River. With this, electricity could be generated. The American bauxite company Alcoa was interested in this plan. In 1958, an agreement was signed between Alcoa and the Surinamese government. Alcoa would build a dam and also a factory to process bauxite in our country. This factory would need most of the generated electricity to smelt the aluminum. The advantage for our country would be more employment and more money could be earned with the export of aluminum.

It was agreed that the dam and the hydroelectric power plant would become property of Suriname only after 75 years. But Alcoa stopped mining bauxite in our country in 2015, and the factory was also closed. And since January 1, 2020, the dam has officially been transferred to Suriname.

In 1960, construction began on the twelve-kilometer-long dam. This dam was not at Brokopondo, but somewhat more to the south, at the village of Afobaka. Hence, the dam is also called the Afobaka Dam.

The Brokopondo Plan was developed by Dutch hydraulic engineer Professor Dr. Ir. van Blommestein, after whom the reservoir is named. Officially, the name is therefore Professor Dr. Ir. W. J. van Blommestein Lake, but it is better known as the Brokopondo Reservoir. Professor Dr. Ir. van Blommestein was also involved in the development of the drainage of the Wageningen Plan, mentioned in the previous lesson.

Due to the construction of the reservoir, a very large area of land was submerged. In this area, there were several Maroon villages, and the people who lived there had to move. About 5,000 people had to leave their homes and living environment. The Surinamese government built new villages for these people, such as Klaaskreek and Brownsweg. These villages are called transmigration villages.

The people did not want to leave their old villages at all, and only when the water began to rise did they have to leave. The houses in the new villages were small, and there were not enough of them. In their new homes, the people did not even get part of the generated electricity for which they should have made space. That electricity went via large cables to Paranam, where an alumina plant and an aluminum smelter had been built.

Not only the people from the area had to move, but also many animals. As the water rose, people sailed back and forth in boats, looking for animals that were trapped by the rising water. With these actions, known as Operation Gwamba, many monkeys, anteaters, deer, and other animals were saved, which would otherwise have drowned.

### Teacher's Guide - Answers and Explanations

Topic 5 – Development of Our Country After 1945
The Brokopondo Dam in the Suriname River

QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS

1. Are the following statements true or false?
   a. The Ten-Year Plan came when the Prosperity Fund had ended. True
   b. The Planning Bureau was established during the Ten-Year Plan. False
   c. During the Prosperity Fund, our country also had to pay part of the costs itself. False
   d. The Planning Bureau was established to develop and execute plans. True
   e. The Ten-Year Plan was developed for 1955 to 1965. True

2. Briefly explain what the Brokopondo Plan involved. What did they want to build and why?
   The Brokopondo Plan involved building a dam and hydroelectric power plant in the Suriname River to generate electricity.

3. Explain why the American bauxite company Alcoa was interested in the Brokopondo Plan.
   The American bauxite company was interested in the Brokopondo Plan because they wanted to set up a factory to process bauxite for which they needed electricity.

4. Fill in what was agreed upon in the Brokopondo agreement.
   Alcoa would build a dam and a factory to process bauxite. This factory needed a lot of electricity to smelt aluminum. The advantage for our country was more employment and we could earn more money.

5. In 1958, the Brokopondo agreement was signed. Name the two parties in signing the Brokopondo agreement.
   1. Alcoa
   2. Surinamese government

6. a. The dam would only become Suriname's after 75 years. Calculate in which year that would be.
   1958 + 75 = 2033
   b. The dam was officially transferred to Suriname on January 1, 2020. Calculate how many years earlier than agreed the dam was transferred to our country.
   2033 - 2020 = 13 years earlier

7. Which answer is correct?
   The official name of the reservoir is:
   a. Alcoa reservoir
   b. Brokopondo reservoir
   c. Professor Dr. Ir. W. J. van Blommestein Lake
   d. Suriname reservoir

8. a. In which river was the reservoir created?
   In the Suriname River
   b. Why did the people who lived along this river have to move?
   Because the area where these people lived was flooded for the construction of the reservoir.

9. Which answer is correct?
   The moving of a whole group of people from one place to another is called:
   a. Emigration
   b. Immigration
   c. Transmigration
   d. Urbanization

10. Explain what Operation Gwamba involved.
    Operation Gwamba involved rescuing the animals that got stuck in trees or on small islands due to the rising water.
