### Student Textbook Content

### Louis Doedel and Anton de Kom

Louis Doedel

To draw attention to the needs of the unemployed, the Committee of Action was established in June 1931. The Committee was led by Louis Doedel. Louis Doedel himself had returned unemployed from Curaçao.

This Committee prepared a plan that they presented to the governor. This plan proposed:

1. To give unemployed people free land to practice agriculture or for gold mining.
2. To provide government support to unemployed people who were willing to work.
3. To provide free food to undernourished children.

The governor promised to work on this plan. But after four months, not much had been done. The governor had established a support committee and issued a number of concessions to mine gold. Both measures had little result. On October 28, 1931, the Surinaamse Volksbond (Surinamese People's Union) organized a meeting to discuss the situation. The need was high and people were starving. People were angry because they felt the government was not doing enough. During this meeting, people could no longer restrain themselves. In protest, they took to the streets. Because people were hungry and desperate, shops and bakeries were plundered. The police acted harshly, resulting in one dead, forty arrests, and many injured. Despite the government's harsh actions, the workers were not prevented from organizing. Louis Doedel continued, together with others, to advocate for workers and the unemployed. But after various actions, he was arrested by the police in 1937 when he wanted to hand over a proposal to the governor. He was locked up in a psychiatric institution without having been convicted by a judge.

It happened quite often that the government had people who protested against the administration detained and observed for a short time. But Louis Doedel was never released again. It seemed as if people had forgotten about him. Only in 1980, when he was old and sick, was he transferred to a retirement home where he died that same year. He was then 75 years old.

Another example of someone who stood up for workers and the unemployed is Anton de Kom. He was born in Paramaribo on February 22, 1898. In Suriname, he worked as an accountant at a balata company. In 1920, he left for the Netherlands. From the Netherlands, he remained in contact with friends in Suriname who stood up for the interests of workers. In 1933, he returned to Suriname with his family.

### Anton de Kom

The situation of workers, as Anton de Kom found it, was very sad. De Kom criticized these abuses and wrote a program that would bring changes to the condition of workers. In this program, he wanted, among other things:

1. A unity movement for all workers and farmers.
2. Introduction of an eight-hour workday, prohibition of child labor, and abolition of contract labor.
3. Independence of Suriname.
4. Freedom of organization for workers and farmers.

His book "We Slaves of Suriname" had great influence in our country. Anton de Kom wanted to give lectures and hold public meetings for the people. But the government forbade this. Therefore, De Kom opened an advice bureau on the grounds of his parental home on Pontewerfstraat. Many people came to him with their complaints. The government did not like any of this and was of the opinion that De Kom was an agitator and troublemaker. Therefore, he was arrested on January 31, 1933, and imprisoned in Fort Zeelandia.

A few days later, a large group of people demanded the release of Anton de Kom. Here, police shot at the people, and there were dead and wounded. De Kom remained in prison, and in May 1933, he was secretly put on a boat and sent to the Netherlands. He was banished from Suriname. In the Netherlands, he supported the resistance against Germany during the Second World War. He was captured by Germany. On April 24, 1945, he died in Germany. Anton de Kom is remembered as a resistance hero.

### REMEMBER

- In 1931, the Committee of Action was established under the leadership of Louis Doedel.
- The Committee of Action presented a relief plan for the unemployed to the governor.
- In October 1931, unemployed people took to the streets in protest because the plan was not implemented by the governor.
- Louis Doedel was arrested by the government in 1937 and locked up in the State Psychiatric Institution.
- Anton de Kom also stood up for the interests of workers and the unemployed. He had prepared a program for this.
- The government forbade Anton de Kom from giving lectures, so he opened an advice bureau on his property.
- In 1933, Anton de Kom was banished from Suriname by the government.

### Teacher's Guide - Answers and Explanations

### QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS

**1.** Which answer is not correct?

The Committee of Action proposed to:

- a. Provide free food to undernourished children.
- b. Abolish contract labor.
- c. Provide government support to unemployed people who were willing to work.
- **d. Give unemployed people land for agriculture or gold mining.**

**2.** On October 28, 1931, unrest broke out after a meeting of unemployed people.

- a. Why did people resort to plundering shops and bakeries?
  - People resorted to plundering shops and bakeries because they were hungry and desperate because the governor had not yet done much.

- b. The government found these plunders brutal. Do you agree or not? Why do you say so?
  - *The answer will vary per student.*

- c. Tell what the government's reaction was to this unrest.
  - The government's reaction was the harsh action of the police, resulting in one dead and many injured, and forty arrests.

**3.** Why do you think the government had Louis Doedel arrested and locked up?
The government had Louis Doedel arrested and locked up because he protested against the government.

**4.** Louis Doedel died in 1980. He was then 75 years old.

- a. In what year was he born?
  - He was born in the year 1905.

- b. How old was he when he was arrested and locked up?
  - He was arrested in 1937. He was then 32 years old.

- c. How many years was he locked up?
  - He was locked up from 1937 to 1980. That is 43 years.

**5.** Briefly tell who Anton de Kom was. Answer the following questions:

- a. When was he born?
  - Anton de Kom was born on February 22, 1898.

- b. In what country was he born?
  - Anton de Kom was born in Paramaribo, Suriname.

- c. Where did he work?
  - He worked as an accountant at a balata company.

- d. What did he want for workers and the unemployed?
  - For workers and the unemployed, he wanted the following:
    1. A unity movement for all workers and farmers.
    2. Introduction of an eight-hour workday, prohibition of child labor, and abolition of contract labor.
    3. Freedom of organization for workers and farmers.

- e. What did he want for Suriname as a country?
  - He wanted independence of Suriname.

**6.** Which answer is not correct? Anton de Kom wrote a program. Proposals included:

- a. A unity movement for workers and farmers.
- b. Introduction of an eight-hour workday.
- **c. Free food for undernourished children.**
- d. Independence of Suriname.

**7.** Why did Anton de Kom open an advice bureau?
Because the government forbade him from giving lectures and holding public meetings for the people. At the advice bureau, people could come to him with their problems.

**8.** Anton de Kom opened the advice bureau on his family's property on Pontewerfstraat. What is this street called today?
Today this street is called the Anton de Komstraat.

**9.** Look up the word "agitate" in the dictionary.

- a. Explain in your own words who is called an agitator.
  - An agitator is someone who incites others to do negative things.

- b. Why did the government see Anton de Kom as an agitator?
  - Because Anton de Kom helped people with their complaints. The government did not like this and was of the opinion that he incited people against the government.
